RINO

01 Robotic platform used

For this work, we use the robotic platform available in the nuScenes dataset: an autonomous-vehicle mobile robot equipped with 6 cameras, 5 radars, and 1 spinning LiDAR. These sensors are distributed to cover a full 360-degree field of view. RINO is developed around camera and/or radar inputs because LiDAR dependence moves away from a deployable setting when memory, inference speed, and hardware cost are considered together.

02 Motivation

3D semantic occupancy estimation uses onboard sensors to predict semantic voxels in 3D space, where each voxel represents a 0.4-meter region and can belong to one of 18 classes. The task is intended to support mobile-robot operation as a perception layer for trajectory planning, control, SLAM, and related autonomy modules. Because it spans multiple perception layers, the system must reason about depth, foreground objects, background structure, and voxel-level semantic segmentation.

03 Research Gap

Although occupancy estimation is well established in robotics and trajectory planning, it was only brought into the autonomous-driving benchmark setting through a CVPR 2023 challenge. The literature still lacks a deployable alternative that reconciles accuracy, low memory, and high inference speed with real-world safety and applicability. RINO targets this gap with a design bias toward practical deployment rather than offline-only performance.

Preliminary metrics

Comparative table with top methods reported in the literature.

Values are extracted from preliminary experiments assembled for this demo. The percentages below were computed by comparing our model with the current state of the art presented at CVPR 2025 (STCOcc). The reported memory was measured with FP16 in the training regime. In inference-only mode, RINO drops to approximately 1.5 GB of VRAM. As shown, RINO is the only model that runs in real time, can be reproduced on a robot with a standard single GPU rather than a cluster, and keeps its metrics at the level of the strongest models.

Status

Work in progress.

We are currently working to reach state-of-the-art metrics while keeping inference above 30 FPS. This trade-off is the core target of the project: accuracy at the level of the strongest models, but with runtime behavior compatible with a deployable robotic platform.

Alongside this experimental push, the manuscript writing stage is ongoing. The final public release is planned for November 2026.
